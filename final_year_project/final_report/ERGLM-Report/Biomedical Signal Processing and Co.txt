Background and Objective: Writing endoscopic image reports requires extensive domain expertise, and even experienced clinicians spend substantial time on them. Although multimodal large language models (MLLMs) excel at cross-modal understanding and generation, general models struggle with specialized terminologies, pathological features, and complex medical logic, limiting their accuracy and applicability in endoscopy. Existing fine-tuning methods can improve model performance to some extent, but they often suffer from limited adaptability and insufficient domain-specific knowledge when applied to tasks in specialized fields.
Methods: We propose a fine-tuning multimodal large Language Model for Endoscopic Report Generation (ERGLM). We apply a two-stage parameter-efficient fine-tuning method to address the specific requirements of endoscopic report generation. In the first-stage fine-tuning, we collect a large number of publicly available datasets to acquire knowledge of digestive endoscopy for addressing the lack of domain-specific knowledge. In the second stage, we use professionally clinical data for fine-tuning to enhance the professionalism of the model in generating reports. Then a prompt-based LoRA fine-tuning method (P-LoRA) is integrated into both stages to better adapt to the task of generating professional endoscopic reports.
Results: Experimental results show that ERGLM attains state-of-the-art performance on our dataset, achieving CIDEr of 0.2667, ROUGE-L of 0.4598, METEOR of 0.4070, BLEU-4 of 0.3863, and BERTScore of 0.8216.
 

 

 
1.	Introduction

In recent years, the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has greatly enhanced the performance of multimodal large language models in integrating visual and textual information. Recent studies have made notable contributions to endoscopic tasks [1,2]. In the medical field, especially in endoscopic examinations, generating high-quality reports is critical for early disease detection, diagnosis, and treatment [3,4]. Endoscopy produces a large amount of image data, which doctors must carefully analyze to write accurate reports. This takes time and expertise, and less experienced doctors may make mistakes. By learning general knowledge from large-scale data, MLLMs can quickly adapt to specific domains and handle the complex mapping between endoscopic images and diagnostic reports [5].
Previous studies [6–8] have focused on building comprehensive multimodal models for medical applications, aiming to achieve in-tegrated reasoning capabilities across modalities. Large-scale medical
 
datasets like PubMed [9] provide abundant general medical knowl-edge but lack detailed annotations for specific diseases or organs. High-quality annotated endoscopic images are especially scarce, lim-iting the development of effective diagnostic models. Additionally, while MLLMs like VisualGLM-6B [10,11] and DeepSeek-VL-7B [12] demonstrate strong general-purpose understanding, their performance in medical applications remains limited, especially in comprehending specialized medical knowledge. Moreover, training multimodal large models from scratch requires vast computational resources. Thus, effi-cient fine-tuning of existing large models has become a more practical approach [13].
LoRA [14] is an example of a reparameterization-based approach. Prefix Tuning [6] is a classic example of prompt-based fine-tuning, while adapter-based methods involve freezing the parameters of the pre-trained model and fine-tuning only newly added adapter structures.

In practice, directly applying general-purpose models and adapting them to specialized medical domains through fine-tuning often fails to fully capture domain-specific knowledge, making the prior injec-tion of specialized knowledge necessary. Although LoRA has demon-strated strong generalizability across various tasks, its fine-tuning per-formance on specialized downstream tasks still leaves room for im-provement [15]. Therefore, effectively leveraging prompt-based meth-ods to enhance LoRA’s capability in generating reports for specialized tasks remains a critical challenge.
Inspired by the aforementioned analyses, we propose a fine-tuning multimodal large Language Model for Endoscopic Report Generation (ERGLM), which achieves state-of-the-art performance in comparisons with mainstream foundational models. To enhance the fine-tuning results, we construct a high-quality multimodal endoscopic dataset. The data is initially annotated by professional physicians and subse-quently templated using GPT-4. We adopt a two-stage P-LoRA fine-tuning method to tackle the knowledge deficiencies commonly encoun-tered during fine-tuning of general-purpose models [16]. In the first stage, we infuse the model with domain-specific medical knowledge, enabling it to comprehend and adapt to the unique requirements of medical tasks, thus bridging the knowledge gap of general models in the medical domain. In the second stage, we guide the model to produce endoscopic reports that align with established medical standards. This staged optimization strategy effectively enhances the model’s capability for generating endoscopic reports.
The contributions of this paper could be summarized as follows:
1.	We propose a fine-tuning multimodal large Language Model for Endoscopic Report Generation(ERGLM), which focuses on opti-mizing foundational large models to meet the specific require-ments of medical endoscopic report generation. Our method improves the accuracy of endoscopic report generation and can effectively assist physicians for diagnosis and report writing.
2.	We propose a two-stage fine-tuning framework using the prompt-based LoRA (P-LoRA) method to adapt DeepSeek-VL-7B for endoscopic report generation. P-LoRA achieves about a 5% improvement over LoRA. A comprehensive evaluation demonstrates that it significantly improves the model’s clinical applicability and diagnostic accuracy.
3.	We develop a GPT-4-based templating pipeline to process endo-scopic report data in order to build a high-quality multimodal endoscopic dataset. Additionally, a cosine distance-based sen-tence selection strategy is employed for rigorous semantic filter-ing, substantially improving the performance of the fine-tuned large model.
4.	We design an endoscopic report measurement score (ERMS) to provide a comprehensive evaluation of generated reports. This metric is intended to quantify the clinical quality of the reports across multiple dimensions, including, but not limited to, completeness of information, factual correctness, and clarity and consistency of medical terminology.

2.	Related work

2.1.	MLLMs

In recent years, the emergence of multimodal large models has profoundly transformed the landscape of multimodal data processing
 
and learning. To address the limitations of pretrained language models in visual-language tasks, Wang et al. [17] proposed CogVLM, which bridges the gap between fixed pretrained language models and image encoders by incorporating trainable visual expert modules into the attention and FFN layers. This allows for deep integration of visual and linguistic features without compromising the performance of nat-ural language processing tasks. Bai et al. [18] proposed the Qwen-VL series of vision-language models to overcome the limitations of large language models in processing visual signals. The models achieved new benchmarks in tasks such as image captioning, question answering, and visual localization through a three-stage training process with multilingual data, demonstrating superior performance in real-world dialogue-based benchmarks. Liu et al. [19] utilized GPT-4 with only language to generate multimodal language-image instruction-following data, introducing LLaVA. LLaVA connects vision encoders with LLMs to enable universal visual and linguistic understanding. It demonstrates multimodal chat capabilities and similar performance to multimodal GPT-4 on unseen images. However, as medical data account for only a limited proportion of their training corpora, general-purpose models exhibit insufficient perception and reasoning capabilities regarding domain-specific medical knowledge. As a result, they often struggle to accurately identify lesion characteristics or generate report content aligned with clinical standards. This limitation is precisely one of the main motivations for proposing ERGLM, which aims to enhance model performance in specialized tasks by incorporating medical knowledge and endoscopic reports.

2.2.	Medical MLLMs

With the advancement of technology, the medical field has accumu-lated massive amounts of multimodal data, including medical imaging, electronic health records, surgical videos, pathological slides, and mul-tidimensional clinical test indicators. This wealth of data provides a solid foundation for constructing multimodal large models [20]. Chen et al. [21] introduced the HuatuoGPT-Vision model, in which they re-fined medical image–text pairs from PubMed and applied MLLM-based denoising to create the PubMedVision dataset, significantly enhancing model performance in medical multimodal tasks. In the domain of gastrointestinal endoscopy, Seenivasan et al. [22] tackled the limitation that GPT-style unidirectional language models cannot directly process surgical images by introducing SurgicalGPT, an end-to-end trainable model that improved accuracy by 3%–5% across three datasets. Ji et al. [23] introduced the ColonINST dataset and the ColonGPT model to address the lack of multimodal benchmarks for colonoscopy and the suboptimal performance of general-purpose large models. The pro-posed model achieved state-of-the-art results in classification, referring expression, and localization tasks, markedly enhancing generalization ability. Schmidgall et al. [24] sought to overcome the limitations of existing surgical VLMs, including task specificity and poor generaliz-ability, and developed GP-VLS, an open-source general-purpose surgical vision–language model that achieved substantial accuracy gains in multiple surgical vision–language tasks.
Although building MLLMs from scratch can deliver strong task-specific performance, it demands vast amounts of annotated data, substantial computational resources, and prolonged training cycles, greatly increasing development costs. Moreover, given the highly spe-cialized and domain-specific nature of medical applications, clinicians often favor models tailored to their discipline. Therefore our study focuses on how to efficiently fine-tune existing general large models for the specialized task of endoscopic medical report generation.

2.3.	Fine-tuning methods

With the success of large-scale pretrained models, these MLLMs have demonstrated exceptional performance across various tasks. How-ever, due to their enormous parameter size, direct training is costly,
 

 
and using the raw models without adaptation for specific tasks cannot optimize their performance. Therefore, fine-tuning has become an es-sential method for adapting general models to specific tasks [25,26]. Li et al. [8] proposed the LLaVA-Med model to address the issue of biomedical image understanding. In the first stage, the PMC-15M dataset was used to align biomedical vocabulary. In the second stage, data generated by GPT-4 was used for end-to-end instruction fine-tuning. He et al. [27] introduced the PeFoMed framework to address parameter efficiency in MLLMs for medical imaging tasks. Through a two-stage fine-tuning strategy, they significantly improved model performance on medical imaging tasks while maintaining parameter efficiency. Chen et al. [28] used generative pretraining and fine-tuning methods to solve the challenges of data privacy and high annotation costs in medical visual question answering tasks. Lu et al. [29] proposed an effective two-stage fine-tuning strategy to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of generating radiology reports from medical images. They improved model performance in multimodal tasks by aligning visual features as soft visual prompts with the text embedding space of large language models. Wu et al. [30] constructed the PathEnhanceDS dataset, which contains 45,000 cases, and used it for instruction tuning to improve the model’s performance in tasks such as pathological image classification, caption generation, and question answering. In summary, although existing medical MLLMs and fine-tuning methods have achieved promising results, they still face several key limitations:
(1) insufficient incorporation of domain-specific medical knowledge
during fine-tuning, leading to limited understanding of clinical seman-tics; (2) a lack of adaptive prompt mechanisms to guide model attention toward task-relevant representations; and (3) inefficiencies arising from uniform LoRA adaptation across all layers. To address these challenges, our proposed ERGLM introduces a two-stage knowledge infusion frame-work that integrates domain knowledge at both pre-adaptation and task-specific fine-tuning stages. Furthermore, by combining adaptive prompts with a selectively applied LoRA structure, ERGLM achieves more efficient parameter utilization and stronger domain adaptability, thereby overcoming the deficiencies of previous approaches.

3.	Method

In this paper, we propose a two-stage P-LoRA method and fine-tune the DeepSeek-VL-7B as the base model, effectively improving its ability to describe endoscopic images. The structure of this chapter is arranged as follows: Section 3.1 provides an overview of the overall framework of our fine-tuned model; Section 3.2 explains the specific process of the two-stage fine-tuning method, where the first stage focuses on learning domain knowledge and the second stage emphasizes generating high-quality reports; Section 3.3 elaborates on the fine-tuning method we adopted, which is more suitable for generating reports from endoscopic images; finally, Section 3.4 introduces our sentence selection method based on cosine similarity to enhance the dataset.

3.1.	Overview

As shown in Fig. 1, we present the overall architectural design for fine-tuning a multimodal large language model using the P-LoRA method. In the first stage, we extensively collect publicly available datasets related to endoscopy, which include endoscopic reports, pre-scriptions, dialogues, and other content. During this phase, we inject domain knowledge into the model using these public datasets and fine-tune the model with the P-LoRA method, while keeping the param-eters of the visual encoder and connector frozen. In the second stage, with the assistance of Qilu Hospital of Shandong University and its expert physicians, we construct a private multimodal dataset contain-ing gastric endoscopic images and corresponding textual descriptions. The visual encoder extracts deep features from gastric images, while the large language model extracts semantic features from the related textual descriptions. Throughout this process, the parameters of the
 
visual encoder and connector remain frozen, allowing the focus to be on fine-tuning the specific parameters of the large language model. This two-stage fine-tuning enhances the model’s understanding of complex multimodal medical data while reducing computational costs, enabling accurate integration of visual and textual features for endoscopic report generation.
We adopt the open-source multimodal large language model DeepSeek-VL-7B [12] as the foundational model. In our approach, DeepSeek-VL-7B is utilized to extract visual features from gastric endo-scopic images and combine them with corresponding textual descrip-tions. 3.2.	Two-stage fine-tuning strategy

General multimodal large language models (MLLMs) exhibit knowl-edge gaps in endoscopy, particularly in understanding specific lesion characteristics and medical terminology. To address this limitation and improve the quality of generated endoscopic reports, we propose a two-stage fine-tuning strategy that explicitly separates domain knowledge acquisition from report generation. This separation allows the model to first internalize core medical knowledge and then adapt to the stylistic and structural conventions of professional endoscopic reports, leading to more accurate and clinically consistent outputs.
In the first stage, PubMed [9] provides rich medical semantics across diverse diseases and organs, serving as a strong foundation for multimodal medical pretraining. To better align with endoscopic tasks, we further extract a subset focused on gastrointestinal descrip-tions. This selected dataset is meticulously filtered to better suit the fine-tuning of models for learning medical knowledge in the gastric domain. MedTrinity-25M [33] is a large-scale multimodal medical dataset comprising approximately 25 million image–text pairs, en-compassing various types of medical imaging and their corresponding clinical descriptions. The dataset integrates medical knowledge from multiple fields, including radiology, pathology, and endoscopy, offering robust support for the training and research of multimodal medical models. VQA-Med [34] is a dataset specifically designed for medical im-age question-answering tasks, combining medical images with relevant clinical questions to facilitate multimodal understanding of medical imaging. By leveraging the extensive domain knowledge available in public datasets for the first-phase fine-tuning, the model can effectively acquire core medical knowledge related to the stomach, including le-sion localization, language expressions of structural changes in tissues, and common terms used in diagnostic reports. During this phase, we freeze the vision encoder and other components, performing limited parameter fine-tuning only on the large language model.
In the second stage, to generate templated language that better aligns with the professional style preferences of physicians, we fine-tune the model using data annotated by physicians from Qilu Hospi-tal of Shandong University. After undergoing specific data processing workflows, this phase focuses on fully leveraging the high level of pro-fessionalism and standardizing language expressions in the physician-annotated data. By further optimizing the model, it can more accurately learn and master commonly used terminology, sentence structures, and expression patterns in endoscopic reports, thereby enhancing its ability to produce outputs consistent with professional medical documentation practices.

By separating domain knowledge learning from professional report generation, the model can follow a clear and structured knowledge transfer process. In the first stage, it focuses on acquiring core medical knowledge such as lesion localization, tissue morphology, and diag-nostic terminology without interference from report language style. In the second stage, the model is fine-tuned on physician-annotated reports to learn sentence structures, templated expressions, and stylis-tic conventions specific to endoscopic documentation. This approach reduces conflicts between factual knowledge and language style during training, supports efficient transfer of medical knowledge to report generation, and enables the model to produce outputs that are both clinically accurate and linguistically coherent. As a result, the two-stage strategy improves the quality, consistency, and reliability of generated endoscopic reports.

3.3.	P-LoRA method

Although LoRA has demonstrated good generality across multiple tasks, its fine-tuning performance on domain-specific downstream tasks still requires improvement [15]. To effectively adapt large multimodal models to the endoscopic domain, we combine soft prompt tuning with selective LoRA fine-tuning. Soft prompt tuning efficiently guides the model to align with domain-specific language patterns without modifying the majority of model parameters, while LoRA provides targeted weight updates that enhance feature extraction from visual and textual inputs. This combination leverages the strengths of both approaches, achieving better domain adaptation than either method alone: prompt tuning alone may not sufficiently adjust deep represen-tations, whereas LoRA alone may require fine-tuning more parameters and increase computational cost. We apply LoRA selectively to the first, middle, and last layers of the model. This strategy is based on the observation that lower layers capture general visual features, middle layers integrate multimodal interactions, and higher layers encode task-specific representations. By updating only these key layers, we achieve a balance between adaptation performance and computational efficiency, allowing effective domain alignment while keeping training cost manageable as illustrated in Fig. 2.
First, trainable prompt parameters [35] are inserted into each atten-tion layer, as shown in Fig. 2(a). The soft prompt tokens are initialized using the same normal distribution as the pretrained embedding layer.

3.4.	Data optimization and filtering

In this experiment, we use a large number of clinical gastroscopy reports collected from Qilu Hospital of Shandong University as the research data. These reports provide detailed records of patients’ gas-tric health, including a large number of gastric endoscopic images that cover key areas such as the esophagus, cardia, fundus, body, antrum, pylorus, duodenal bulb, and descending segment. These images not only provide intuitive visual information for our research but are also crucial for accurately assessing the condition of the stom-ach. Additionally, the reports include detailed descriptions written by skilled physicians, based on their clinical experience and profes-sional knowledge, providing a thorough record and analysis of gastric
 

 
Templatizing textual sentences helps unify the structure of input data and standardize its formatting, thereby effectively reducing data noise caused by inconsistent expression. For complex medical texts, the application of standardized templates is particularly critical: it ensures that the model focuses on extracting key clinical features during the fine-tuning phase, while avoiding interference from non-uniform descriptive styles across different reports. To implement this templa-tization, we first provide GPT-4 with a structured positive prompt: You are a professional medical text processing assistant. Your task is to convert the following endoscopic image description sentence into a standardized template format: ‘‘This image shows (gastric region). It describes the following features (details of color, texture, presence)’’. To ensure methodological transparency and reproducibility, we explic-itly define the interaction protocol. First, raw sentences are provided to GPT-4 with prompts for template conversion. Next, the generated outputs are reviewed by experienced gastroenterologists, who verify medical accuracy, correct terminology usage, and check logical con-sistency. Finally, low-quality or inconsistent outputs are revised or discarded. Through this multi-step process, GPT-4 serves as an auxiliary tool for annotation, while expert physicians guarantee clinical validity and professional reliability.
During the optimization process, a core principle must always be followed: the optimized sentence must remain semantically consistent with the original sentence, ensuring that the conveyed information is not altered or lost. To this end, introducing a reliable semantic consistency judgment method is crucial. This not only helps assess the semantic alignment between the optimized sentence and the original sentence, providing quality assurance for the optimization process, but also strikes a balance between richness and accuracy in text expression. 4.	Experiments and discussion

To validate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conducted fine-tuning on both public and private datasets and analyzed and compared the experimental results. The structure of this chapter is organized as follows: Section 4.1 describes the datasets employed; Section 4.2 provides a detailed description of the parameters and equipment used in the experiments; Section 4.3 introduces the key evaluation metrics adopted in the experiments; and Section 4.4 presents an in-depth analysis of the experimental results, including comparative experiments with different models to verify the effectiveness of our fine-tuned model in generating endoscopic reports, the advantages of the fine-tuning method, and the efficacy of the two-stage fine-tuning approach. 4.1.	Datasets

We adopt a two-phase fine-tuning approach, where the first phase screens gastrointestinal descriptive data from open-source medical datasets, as shown in Table 1. At this stage, we use a gastroen-terology medical vocabulary as the basis to conduct comprehensive screening and precise filtering of the data, ensuring that the final dataset centers on core knowledge in the field of gastroenterology. Irrelevant information is effectively removed, achieving a high degree of alignment between the dataset content and the target domain. After the initial screening process, each report is independently reviewed by five experienced gastroenterologists. They examine the accuracy of medical terminology, the standardization of lesion descriptions, and the rigor of medical logic in the data. The final label of each report is determined by a majority voting strategy: if at least three experts judge the report as meeting clinical standards, it is labeled as compliant; otherwise, it is labeled as non-compliant. For reports labeled as non-compliant, a discussion is conducted to determine the final label. In addition, the physicians filter out low-quality or unusable samples, further enhancing the dataset’s professional reliability and clinical ap-plicability. These datasets, including PubMed, encompass rich content
 
across general medicine, providing detailed descriptions of various body parts and diseases. With their large scale and comprehensive semantic knowledge, these datasets are valuable resources for medical research and model training. By filtering and cleaning high-relevance gastrointestinal data from PubMed and other datasets, we obtained a subset of approximately 60K records for the first phase of fine-tuning. This dataset not only includes detailed problem descriptions but also contains extensive domain knowledge, enabling the model to learn foundational semantic knowledge, lesion descriptions, and anatomical expressions related to gastrointestinal medicine, thereby laying a solid groundwork for subsequent training.
In the second stage, we use a large number of gastroscopy reports collected from Qilu Hospital of Shandong University as research data. These reports are strictly screened and processed using the method described in Section 3.4, resulting in a more targeted training dataset. The data are derived from real clinical patients and have undergone de-identification processing to ensure compliance and ethical use by pre-processing information involving patient privacy. 4.3.	Evaluation metrics

A large number of previous studies [39–41] adopt metrics such as CIDEr, ROUGE, and METEOR to evaluate the similarity between generated medical descriptions and the original descriptions. CIDEr (Consensus-based Image Description Evaluation) [42] is specifically designed for image description tasks. CIDEr aligns more closely with human judgment when determining the similarity between two sen-tences because it employs TF-IDF to assign different weights to vari-ous n-grams. Intuitively, frequently occurring phrases are given lower weights, while less common phrases are considered more distinctive, drawing more attention from people to these unique words.
ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) [43] is a metric used for automatically evaluating the quality of generated text. It is commonly applied in tasks such as summarization, machine trans-lation, and text generation. ROUGE assesses the quality of generated text by measuring the overlap between the generated and reference texts, with a primary focus on recall.
METEOR (Metric for Evaluation of Translation with Explicit OR-dering) [44] is a metric used to evaluate the quality of text outputs in tasks such as machine translation, image caption generation, and other natural language generation tasks. It is based on precise matching and semantic similarity between sentences, addressing shortcomings of traditional metrics like BLEU [45], which are less sensitive to word order and semantic similarities.
Therefore, the metrics CIDEr, ROUGE, and METEOR each reflect the similarity between generated endoscopy reports and reference re-ports from different perspectives. CIDEr effectively evaluates whether the generated report accurately preserves key clinical terms, thereby reflecting its fidelity in terms of information importance. ROUGE-L considers the continuity of the longest common subsequence, making it suitable for assessing the quality of structured text in medical reports. METEOR incorporates semantic similarity, word order, and morpho-logical variations, enabling it to more accurately identify semantically equivalent sentences, aligning with clinicians’ standards for ‘‘consis-tency of meaning’’ in reports. However, these metrics mainly focus on linguistic form and are limited in capturing the clinical correctness and practical utility of the reports. Therefore, to further assess the clinical validity and quality of the generated reports, we introduce expert human evaluation.

4.4.4.	Evaluation of the accuracy and quality of generated reports
To assess the clinical accuracy and practical usability of the gen-erated endoscopic reports, we conduct a structured physician-based evaluation. Seven senior endoscopists (each with more than 10 years of clinical experience in gastrointestinal endoscopy) are invited as expert reviewers. All reviewers participate voluntarily and independently, and
 

 
they are blinded to the model identity and experimental settings to avoid potential bias. Each endoscopist independently evaluates the generated reports based solely on the corresponding endoscopic images, without access to reference reports or other reviewers’ judgments. Two aspects are assessed: (1) report accuracy, and (2) overall report quality, measured by the proposed Endoscopy Report Measure Score (ERMS). Here, report accuracy refers to whether all the content described in the report aligns with actual medical facts. If all content in a report is accurate, it is marked as ‘‘Correct’’. If there is any factual error or description inconsistent with clinical reality, the report is marked as ‘‘Incorrect’’. In cases where the evaluations of the seven experts are not consistent, we adopt a majority voting strategy to determine the final label of report accuracy. Specifically, if at least four experts judge a report as ‘‘Correct’’, it is labeled as correct; otherwise, it is labeled as incorrect. This evaluation criterion emphasizes the rigor of the report content and serves as an important indicator for determining whether the report is suitable for real clinical scenarios.
To more comprehensively quantify the quality of the generated reports, the endoscopy report measure score is designed to evaluate the clinical value of a report from multiple dimensions, including but not limited to: completeness of information (whether key lesion char-acteristics and examination elements are covered), factual correctness (whether the content is truthful, objective, and free of hallucinations), and clarity and consistency of medical terminology. Each dimension reflects an essential aspect of clinical utility: completeness ensures that important findings are not omitted; factual correctness guarantees the report aligns with actual patient conditions; and clarity ensures that the report is interpretable and actionable by clinicians. By aggregating scores across multiple expert evaluators, ERMS provides a rigorous, standardized metric for evaluating the clinical reliability and usability of generated reports. The ERMS is calculated as the average of all scores given by all physicians across all reports.
The ERMS score is based on a 5-point Likert scale [27], with the evaluation criteria defined as follows:
•	4 points: The report is highly complete, factually clear, accu-rately expressed, and holds high clinical value.
•	3 points: The report is basically complete, reliably described, with only minor expression flaws that do not affect understand-ing.
•	2 points: The report contains some omissions or vague language that slightly impacts clinical judgment.
•	1 point: The report is incomplete, contains factual deviations or hallucinated content, and has low clinical reference value.
•	0 point: The report is severely flawed or lacks basic medical logic and is not clinically applicable.
After the seven senior endoscopists complete independent evalua-tions, we further examine the consistency among their assessments to ensure the reliability of the human evaluation results. Specifically, for the indicator of report accuracy, we aggregate the judgments of the seven experts for each report into a count matrix and apply Fleiss’ Kappa coefficient for consistency testing. This metric measures the level of agreement among multiple raters in classification tasks, with values ranging from [−1, 1], where 0 indicates agreement equivalent to chance and 1 indicates perfect agreement. Our calculation yields a Fleiss’ Kappa coefficient of 0.783, which corresponds to a substantial level of agreement. We conduct assessments of face validity and content validity. In the face validity evaluation, we invite senior endoscopists to review the ERMS scoring items and provide feedback on whether each item is intuitively appropriate and capable of measuring report qual-ity (clarity, completeness, factual correctness). The experts generally agree that the ERMS evaluation dimensions are reasonable, intuitive, and consistent with clinical judgment habits. In the content validity evaluation, the same panel rates each ERMS item (completeness, factual correctness, terminology clarity) on relevance and representativeness.

The proposed ERGLM outperforms this human baseline in both accuracy and report quality, suggesting that the model-generated reports are not only com-parable to but in some aspects superior to those written by junior doctors. We conduct a short-term clinical evaluation in which real endoscopic images captured during routine examinations are imported into the ERGLM system for report generation. During the test, junior doctors also write reports manually, while experienced endoscopists review and edit the automatically generated reports. Meanwhile, we perform a cost–benefit analysis focusing on time efficiency and work-load reduction. Junior doctors typically take about 3–5 min to write an endoscopic report, whereas ERGLM generates a report automatically within approximately 5–7 s. The evaluation shows that the system can process every image, achieving a 100% edit rate. However, omissions occasionally occur in key regions, such as the gastric antrum or body, suggesting that these areas should be specifically highlighted. We de-sign a three-level satisfaction scale (satisfied, neutral, dissatisfied) for the participating physicians to evaluate the reports. The results show that nearly 75% of the reports are rated as satisfied, approximately 15% as neutral, and 10% as dissatisfied, demonstrating the system’s potential. Clinicians also provide qualitative feedback. They report that the generated reports are generally clear and clinically meaning-ful, especially for common lesions. However, the model sometimes
 

 
offers vague descriptions of subtle mucosal abnormalities and may miss secondary findings. Clinicians also note the need for more precise anatomical localization and more standardized terminology. Overall, they consider the system promising as an assistive tool but indicate that further refinement is required before clinical use.
ERGLM also assists junior doctors in clinical diagnosis. For example, when a junior doctor examines a patient and suspects early gastric cancer but is uncertain, ERGLM correctly identifies the lesion as early gastric cancer, providing additional diagnostic support and reducing decision-making time. This greatly reduces the time and manual effort required for report writing, not only improving clinical efficiency but also allowing physicians to devote more time to diagnosis and patient communication.
To further evaluate how well the model’s confidence aligns with its actual predictive accuracy, we conducted a calibration analysis using the Expected Calibration Error (ECE). Token-level confidence was estimated by averaging the predicted probabilities of the generated report tokens, and these confidence values were grouped into 10 bins to compute the gap between predicted confidence and empirical accuracy. Our model achieved an ECE of 0.042, indicating good calibration performance. The model’s predicted confidence aligns closely with its actual correctness, indicating that the generated diagnostic descriptions are reliable.

4.4.5.	Multidimensional evaluation of generated reports
Previous studies [27,53] have utilized GPT-4 for auxiliary evalu-ation in text generation tasks involving large models. These works leverage GPT-4’s capabilities in natural language understanding and generation to perform in-depth analysis and comprehensive assess-ments of generated text quality. By evaluating the semantic consistency, linguistic fluency, logical coherence, and relevance to reference texts, these studies have effectively enhanced the objectivity and accuracy of evaluations. To comprehensively assess our model’s sensitivity in endoscopic image descriptions, we conduct a multi-dimensional anal-ysis encompassing shape, color, location, size, degree, and texture. To achieve this, we design three complementary evaluation methods: key-word extraction evaluation, human evaluation, and GPT-4 evaluation. The keyword extraction evaluation focuses on the literal similarity between the predicted keywords and the ground truth. However, we also find that this method has limitations when dealing with seman-tically similar expressions that differ in wording. Table 7 presents examples of keywords extracted across different dimensions, which serve as the basis for our comprehensive evaluation across these six dimensions. During the evaluation process, we observe cases where descriptions in the ‘‘shape’’ dimension, such as ‘‘tongue-shaped’’, appear in the ground truth, while the prediction sometimes generates terms like ‘‘leaf-shaped’’. Although these descriptions differ in specific details, they exhibit a degree of similarity and can be considered correct in terms of description. These situations may lead to underestimated scores. Therefore, we extend the evaluation by going beyond literal matching and incorporating semantic similarity into the assessment. To mitigate the impact of semantic ambiguity and prevent descrip-tions generated by the model with potential hallucinations from being mistakenly judged as accurate, we introduce human evaluation and GPT-4-based evaluation as correction methods.
Human evaluation is conducted by seven domain experts who assess the correctness of knowledge, while GPT-4 assists in evaluating the plausibility and semantic consistency of the predicted keywords based on its natural language understanding capabilities. We pay particular attention to identifying potential hallucinations in the generated text to enhance the safety and scientific rigor of the evaluation. As shown in Fig. 7, the accuracy of human-assisted evaluation is very close to that of GPT-4 evaluation, indicating a high degree of consistency between the two methods in verifying the correctness of the keywords. 5.	Limitations and future work

This paper focuses on addressing the problem of automated de-scriptions of endoscopic images while also opening new directions for the application of multimodal foundational models in the medical field. However, many key issues in the field of digestive endoscopy still require further exploration and solutions. For example, achieving more precise automatic detection of lesion locations, improving the accuracy of gastric cancer grading and classification, and optimizing the recognition and diagnosis of complex lesions remain significant challenges.
Although our model demonstrates high accuracy in generating en-doscopic reports, errors of varying clinical severity can occur. High-severity errors include omissions of critical findings, such as early-stage gastric cancer or active bleeding, which may directly impact diag-nosis and treatment decisions. Low-severity errors primarily involve minor wording or stylistic inconsistencies that do not significantly affect clinical interpretation. Expert review shows that most errors fall into the low-severity category, while high-severity errors are rel-atively rare but require particular attention to ensure patient safety. These findings emphasize the need for qualified clinicians to review all model-generated reports and highlight the importance of careful integration of AI tools into clinical workflows. Although our dataset is carefully constructed and annotated, it is limited to a single hos-pital and evaluated by a relatively small group of clinical experts, which may constrain the generalizability of error severity assessment. Although the endoscopic equipment in this hospital covers multiple brands, the same brand may differ across hospitals, and variations in physicians’ expertise may also lead to differences in reports. This single-center data source may restrict the model’s generalizability across
 
different hospitals and clinical environments. In practice, variations in endoscopic imaging equipment, illumination conditions, and color calibration between hospitals can alter image characteristics, while differences in reporting styles, terminology, and diagnostic criteria among physicians may lead to textual inconsistencies. Such physician dependent annotation styles may introduce implicit bias during train-ing, potentially affecting the phrasing, emphasis, or level of diagnostic certainty in the generated reports. Furthermore, regional differences in disease prevalence and case complexity may introduce additional bias. These factors can cause domain shifts that reduce the model’s performance when applied to unseen data. If endoscopic images present ambiguous or contradictory visual cues, the model is designed to gen-erate conservative, evidence-based descriptions rather than definitive diagnoses. This approach helps prevent over-interpretation and main-tains clinical reliability. In addition, automatically flagging instances with high uncertainty for closer physician review represents a highly valuable direction for future work.
Future work will involve a concrete multi-center validation plan. Specifically, we plan to collaborate with several hospitals in different regions of China to collect diverse endoscopic image–report pairs from various imaging systems and patient populations. The evaluation will include cross-hospital transfer tests, consistency assessments across different endoscopic brands, and clinical validation by independent physicians. This study primarily focuses on the analysis of individual endoscopic images, where diagnostic conclusions are limited to describ-ing specific lesions observed within each image. However, in real-world clinical practice, endoscopists typically acquire multiple images per patient to form a comprehensive diagnostic assessment. Accordingly, an important direction for future work is to extend the current single-image interpretation framework toward multi-image and patient-level

Building upon this direction, future research will further explore video-based endoscopy analysis by modeling temporal information across consecutive frames. Additionally, regional differences in medical practices and terminology may lead to interpretative biases in the generated med-ical descriptions. When deploying the proposed model in new hospitals or clinical settings, domain shifts may arise due to differences in endoscopic devices, imaging proto-cols, patient populations, and report-writing styles. To address this issue, several lightweight adaptation strategies can be adopted. First, parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods can be applied using a small number of locally collected image–report pairs, enabling rapid domain adaptation with limited computational cost. Second, prompt adjust-ment provides a flexible alternative, where hospital-specific reporting templates, terminology preferences, or anatomical naming conventions can be incorporated into structured prompts without updating model parameters. In addition, continuous feedback from clinicians can be used to iteratively refine prompts or select representative samples for incremental fine-tuning. These strategies allow the model to be efficiently adapted to new hospitals while preserving data privacy and minimizing deployment overhead.
These challenges not only place higher demands on intelligent
analysis of medical images but also pose significant tests to the model’s multimodal fusion ability and cross-domain generalization. Regarding the application of large language models in healthcare, the foremost challenge lies in data availability. Collecting endoscopic data is time-consuming and costly, so it is important to explore how LLM-based approaches can generate synthetic data that closely reflect real clinical scenarios while adhering to ethical standards [54,55]. Furthermore, in terms of LLM architecture, an important future direction is to design models that effectively leverage the strengths of both language and visual encoders, achieving efficient multimodal feature fusion [56]. Research on clinical endoscopy videos also holds greater practical significance and applicability [57]. Finally, direct inference with LLMs may not fully exploit their potential; existing strategies such as rethink-ing [58] or chain-of-thought reasoning may enhance their reasoning capabilities, representing another highly valuable direction for future work. We are actively communicating with the collaborating hospital to seek approval for releasing a de-identified subset of the dataset. While public release depends on institutional review and privacy regulations, we aim to make as much of the data as ethically permissible publicly accessible. In addition, the pretrained model, P-LoRA configurations, and training scripts will be made available upon publication to support reproducibility and further research.

6.	Conclusion

In this study, we develop an endoscopic image interpretation model using a prompt-based LoRA fine-tuning method, aiming to fully explore
 
and utilize the potential of existing multimodal foundational models to achieve precise medical image interpretation through a two-stage fine-tuning strategy. In the first stage, we systematically align the foun-dational model with domain knowledge by collecting and integrating existing public endoscopic domain knowledge, making it more tailored to the contextual features of endoscopic images. In the second stage, we conduct specialized high-quality annotations using a large amount of clinical data collected by local senior physicians, thereby fine-tuning the model to enhance its medical interpretation ability for endoscopic images. In addition, to ensure factual consistency and reduce hal-lucinations in the generated reports, the model incorporates several reliability-oriented mechanisms. A constrained decoding strategy with a temperature of 0 enforces deterministic and evidence-grounded out-puts. Image–text alignment verification is introduced to ensure that generated descriptions correspond to visually supported regions. Fur-thermore, a structured report format guides the generation process, re-stricting unsupported free-form content and maintaining clinical logic. These strategies collectively enhance the factual grounding and relia-bility of the model’s outputs. Although this study focuses on upper gas-trointestinal endoscopy, the proposed framework is modality-agnostic and can be readily extended to other endoscopic modalities, such as colonoscopy and bronchoscopy, by adapting domain-specific prompts and fine-tuning with corresponding image and report data.
After fine-tuning, the model shows remarkable performance in
generating descriptions of endoscopic images, with particularly high sensitivity to recognizing image morphological features. Specifically, its evaluation metrics include a CIDEr score of 0.2667, ROUGE-L of 0.4598, METEOR of 0.4070, BLEU-4 of 0.3863, and BERTScore of
0.8216. The accuracy of the generated reports reaches 84.03%, which represents a significant improvement compared to its performance be-fore fine-tuning and is comparable to the standard of intermediate-level physicians. In the task of interpreting endoscopic reports, our model significantly outperforms models such as GPT-4V and HuatuoGPT-Vision-7B. To further validate the reliability of automatic evaluation metrics, we compared them with human evaluation results, including report accuracy and the Endoscopy Report Measure Score (ERMS). Overall, the trends of automatic metrics are largely consistent with hu-man assessments: reports scoring higher on CIDEr, ROUGE-L, METEOR, and BLEU also tend to receive higher ERMS and accuracy ratings from expert reviewers. Minor discrepancies are observed in cases where the generated text captures clinically relevant details not fully reflected in n-gram-based metrics, highlighting the complementary value of human evaluation in assessing clinical quality and factual correctness. It should be noted that the above conclusions are drawn under standard endo-scopic acquisition conditions, in which images are of sufficient quality, properly focused, and captured in accordance with routine clinical protocols. When images are missing, severely degraded, or affected by artifacts such as motion blur, occlusion, or inadequate illumination, the accuracy and completeness of the generated reports may be com-promised, potentially resulting in less precise descriptions or omitted findings. In this work, basic robustness considerations are incorpo-rated through structured generation constraints, while future studies will further explore image quality assessment and uncertainty-aware mechanisms to enhance reliability in challenging clinical scenarios. This level of accuracy is comparable to the standard of intermediate-level physicians. It accurately captures subtle visual characteristics and generates high-quality descriptions that meet medical professional standards, providing a solution with practical application potential in the field of endoscopic image interpretation.
